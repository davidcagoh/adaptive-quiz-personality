Here's what you have and why each file is structured the way it is.
ipip_big5_50item.json — the Goldberg (1992) 10-item-per-factor markers. Each item carries factor, keying (+/−), mbti_dimension, and agree_indicates (what a high response signals in MBTI terms). The metadata block at the top gives you the scoring rule, alphas, and the MBTI proxy notes. This is your lean starting point — short enough to administer whole, well-validated, directly comparable to published research.
ipip_neo_300item.json — the full Goldberg (1999) 30-facet instrument. Each item carries facet, facet_name, domain, alpha (facet-level), keying, and agree_indicates_mbti. The metadata documents every domain's facets and flags which facets are best for each MBTI dimension. This is your item bank for the adaptive system — you select from it rather than administer it wholesale.
ipip_explainer.md — the reference document. It covers scoring rules, the full mapping table with known weaknesses, which items to consider dropping (the O6 Liberalism and N5 Immoderation facets), a recommended 80-item adaptive subset (8 high-alpha facets, 2 per MBTI dimension), the calibration dataset URL (OpenPsychometrics, ~1M responses), and all primary citations including the McCrae & Costa (1989) paper that established the Big Five ↔ MBTI mapping in the first place.
The single most important thing to keep in mind as you build: Agreeableness runs backwards relative to T/F. High Agreeableness = Feeling, which means every Agreeableness +keyed item is a −1.0 weight on the T pole, not +1.0. That inversion trips up almost every implementation I've seen.
